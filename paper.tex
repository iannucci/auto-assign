\begin{abstract}
During disaster response, Emergency Services Workers (ESWs) must visit assigned addresses to assess damage. Minimizing the worst-case worker walking distance while balancing workloads is critical for rapid response. We model this as a balanced partitioning problem over \emph{road-network segments} --- uninterrupted stretches of roadway between intersections --- with walking distances constrained to the street network. Each segment carries a precomputed cost matrix that captures all traversal options as a function of entry/exit point, street width, and address distribution. We evaluate three partitioning strategies across 11 neighborhoods in Palo Alto, California (54--1,737 addresses, 11--600 road segments). The right-hand-rule chain achieves the lowest worst-case walking distance in 59\% of test cases, producing assignments within 1.6--2.8$\times$ of the theoretical ideal. Unproductive walking (inter-segment transitions) accounts for 24--59\% of total distance, identifying it as the dominant optimization target. We recommend an oracle approach that runs all strategies and selects the best in under one second.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
disaster response, geographic partitioning, vehicle routing, emergency services, road network, address assignment
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}

Post-disaster damage assessment requires Emergency Services Workers (ESWs) to physically visit assigned addresses, inspect structures, and report conditions via mobile devices. The speed of this process directly affects emergency response effectiveness~\cite{fema2023}.

In the ES-Link system~\cite{eslink}, a Neighborhood Preparedness Coordinator (NPC) assigns addresses to ESWs through a web-based map interface. Manual assignment is tedious and does not optimize for worker travel distance. We developed an auto-assignment algorithm that distributes addresses across $N$ workers, minimizing the worst-case walking distance while maintaining balanced workloads.

The problem combines elements of the Capacitated Vehicle Routing Problem (CVRP)~\cite{toth2014} and balanced graph partitioning~\cite{andreev2006}. Unlike classical VRP, our ``vehicles'' (ESWs on foot) start from arbitrary positions, there is no depot, and the primary constraint is workload balance rather than vehicle capacity. The general problem is NP-hard~\cite{garey1979}, so we seek practical heuristics that produce good solutions in real-time.

A key constraint distinguishes our problem from the standard VRP: \textbf{ESWs must walk on streets.} They cannot jump between non-adjacent addresses, cut through properties, or teleport across blocks. This means straight-line (haversine) distance --- the standard metric in geographic partitioning --- is an unreliable proxy for actual walking distance. Our formulation uses road-network distances throughout.

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
\item[\textbf{C1.}] A road-network segment model that decomposes streets into atomic walkable units between intersections, with addresses snapped to segments and classified by side of street (Section~II-A).
\item[\textbf{C2.}] A per-segment cost matrix that captures all traversal options (through-walk, out-and-back, zigzag, U-turn) as a function of entry/exit port and address geometry, subsuming the traditional U-turn heuristic as an emergent property (Section~II-B).
\item[\textbf{C3.}] A walking metric that decomposes total distance into \emph{productive} (within-segment) and \emph{unproductive} (inter-segment transition) components, enabling direct comparison to the theoretical ideal (Section~II-C).
\item[\textbf{C4.}] Empirical evaluation of three partitioning strategies across 11 neighborhoods showing that the right-hand-rule chain wins 59\% of cases and achieves 1.6--2.8$\times$ overhead versus the theoretical ideal (Section~IV).
\item[\textbf{C5.}] An oracle algorithm that runs all strategies and selects the best in under one second, guaranteeing optimal results across all neighborhood geometries (Section~III-F).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Problem Formulation}
%% ==================================================================

\subsection{Road Network Model (C1)}

We model the street network as a graph $G = (V, E)$ extracted from OpenStreetMap, where vertices $V$ are \emph{intersections} (nodes shared by two or more road ways, or way endpoints) and edges $E$ are \emph{segments} --- uninterrupted stretches of roadway between intersections.

\begin{description}
\item[Segment.] A maximal chain of road edges between intersection nodes, with street name, road class, polyline geometry, and length $D$. Within a segment, no turns are possible; turns occur only at endpoints.
\item[Sub-segment.] One side of a segment. Each segment yields up to two sub-segments (left and right of the centerline), determined by the cross product of the road direction vector and the address displacement vector. A sub-segment contains addresses ordered by fractional position $t_i \in [0,1]$ along the segment.
\end{description}

ESWs are constrained to walk along streets. The three legal moves are: (1) walk to the next address on the same side of a segment, (2) cross the street to an address on the opposite side (cost $\approx w$, the street width), and (3) turn a corner at an intersection to enter an adjacent segment. ESWs may not jump over houses or pass through properties.

\subsection{Segment Cost Matrix (C2)}

Each segment endpoint has two \emph{ports} (one per sidewalk), giving four ports: $S_L, S_R, E_L, E_R$ (start-left, start-right, end-left, end-right). We precompute a $4 \times 4$ cost matrix $\mathbf{C}$ giving the minimum walking distance for every (entry port, exit port) combination.

\textbf{Definitions.} Let $D$ be the segment length (meters), $w$ the street width (estimated from OSM road class: residential $\approx 10$\,m, service $\approx 6$\,m), $n$ the number of addresses, and $r$ the number of \emph{runs} --- maximal consecutive groups of addresses on the same side when sorted by position.

\textbf{Through-traversal} (enter at $S$, exit at $E$ or vice versa). The ESW walks the full segment, crossing the street at each run boundary. The cost is:
\begin{equation}
C(S_s, E_e) = D + k(s,e) \cdot w
\label{eq:through}
\end{equation}
where $k(s,e)$ counts the number of street crossings: $(r-1)$ mandatory run-boundary crossings, plus one if the entry side $s$ differs from the first run's side, plus one if the exit side $e$ differs from the last run's side. The Iverson bracket notation: $k = (r-1) + [s \neq s_1] + [e \neq s_r]$.

\textbf{Out-and-back} (enter and exit at the same end). The ESW walks to the farthest address and returns. The return leg carries no unvisited addresses, so requires zero crossings:
\begin{equation}
C(S_s, S_e) = 2 \cdot t_n \cdot D + k_{\text{out}}(s) \cdot w
\label{eq:outback}
\end{equation}
where $t_n$ is the position of the last address (farthest from $S$). The exit side $e$ is free --- the ESW returns on whichever sidewalk is convenient.

\textbf{Significance.} The cost matrix eliminates the need for a separate ``split or merge'' decision for each segment. When addresses alternate densely between sides ($r$ large), through-traversal is expensive ($D + r \cdot w$) and the optimizer naturally routes the ESW down one side and back --- the classical U-turn pattern. When addresses group by side ($r$ small), through-traversal with few crossings is cheapest. The matrix selects the optimal strategy automatically.

\textbf{Example.} Consider a 200\,m residential segment ($w = 10$\,m) with 20 addresses alternating between sides ($r = 20$). Through-traversal: $200 + 19 \times 10 = 390$\,m. U-turn equivalent (one side $S \to E$, cross, return $E \to S$): $2 \times 200 + 10 = 410$\,m. Here zigzag wins by 5\%. But for a 100\,m segment with the same 20 alternating addresses: through $= 290$\,m vs.\ U-turn $= 210$\,m --- U-turn wins by 28\%. In our data, 97\% of segments prefer zigzag; only 3\% prefer U-turn.

\subsection{Walking Metric (C3)}
\label{sec:metric}

The total walking distance for ESW $k$, assigned an ordered sequence of segments $P_k = (seg_1, seg_2, \ldots, seg_m)$ with chosen ports, is:

\begin{equation}
W_k = \underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^{m} C(\text{in}_i, \text{out}_i)}_{\displaystyle W_k^{\text{prod}}} + \underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^{m-1} d_{\text{road}}(\text{out}_i, \text{in}_{i+1})}_{\displaystyle W_k^{\text{unprod}}}
\label{eq:walk}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $C(\text{in}_i, \text{out}_i)$ is the cost of traversing segment $i$ from entry port $\text{in}_i$ to exit port $\text{out}_i$, read from the segment's precomputed cost matrix. This is \textbf{productive walking}: visiting addresses.
\item $d_{\text{road}}(\text{out}_i, \text{in}_{i+1})$ is the shortest-path distance along the road network from exit port of segment $i$ to entry port of segment $i{+}1$. This is \textbf{unproductive walking}: traveling between segments without visiting any addresses.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Theoretical ideal.} If all $A$ addresses could be distributed with zero unproductive walking and perfect balance, each ESW would walk exactly:
\begin{equation}
W^* = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{\text{all segments}} \min_{\text{in},\text{out}} C(\text{in}, \text{out})
\label{eq:ideal}
\end{equation}

This is unachievable because geographic partitioning inevitably creates boundaries that require inter-segment transitions. The \textbf{overhead ratio} $\rho = W_{\max} / W^*$ measures how close the best assignment comes to the ideal. A ratio of 1.0 would mean zero unproductive walking and perfect balance; in practice we observe $\rho$ between 1.6 and 2.8.

\textbf{Objective.} Minimize the worst-case walking distance:
\begin{equation}
\min_{\{P_1,\ldots,P_N\}} \max_{k \in \{1..N\}} W_k
\label{eq:objective}
\end{equation}
subject to address-count balance: $\max_k |P_k| / \min_k |P_k| \leq \beta$, where $\beta \approx 1.0$.

\subsection{Data}

Segments are extracted from OpenStreetMap via \texttt{osm2pgsql} (Geofabrik California PBF, 1.3\,GB) filtered to Palo Alto ($37.35$--$37.48^\circ$N, $122.05$--$122.20^\circ$W). We extract 64,482 road segments from 54,854 intersection nodes. Addresses come from the City of Palo Alto municipal database (41,857 addresses snapped to 7,430 segments; only 3 addresses exceed the 200\,m snap threshold). Road-network shortest paths are precomputed via Dijkstra between all intersection nodes within each neighborhood.

\section{Algorithms}
%% ==================================================================

All algorithms operate on the segment graph: segments are nodes with four ports and precomputed cost matrices; edges between segments carry road-network distances between shared intersection nodes.

\subsection{Strategy A: Nearest-Neighbor (NN) Chain}

Build a single-ESW walk order using nearest-neighbor on ports: from the current exit port, select the unvisited segment whose (entry port, exit port) combination minimizes transition cost plus segment cost. Partition the resulting chain into $N$ groups at address-count boundaries $\lceil A/N \rceil$. Repeat from all possible starting segments; keep the best.

\subsection{Strategy B: Right-Hand-Rule (RH) Chain}

A variant of Strategy~A. After exiting a segment, score candidates by:
\begin{equation}
\text{score}(s) = d_{\text{road}}(\text{exit}, \text{entry}_s) + 0.5 \cdot \theta_{\text{cw}} + 0.1 \cdot C(\text{in}_s, \text{out}_s)
\label{eq:rh-score}
\end{equation}
where $\theta_{\text{cw}}$ is the clockwise turn angle from the current heading. This produces a spiral traversal that covers contiguous geographic areas (Fig.~\ref{fig:rh-path}).

\subsection{Strategy C: Recursive Bisection}

Split segments by centroid along the longer geographic axis. Recurse until $N$ groups remain. Within each group, build an NN chain (Strategy~A) to order the segments.

\subsection{Intra-Segment Traversal}

Within each segment, the optimal traversal is determined by the cost matrix. Fig.~\ref{fig:uturn-path} illustrates the two extremes: zigzag (few crossings, addresses grouped by side) and U-turn (many crossings, addresses alternating). The cost matrix selects the cheaper option automatically --- no separate heuristic is needed.

\subsection{Scoring Function}

Candidate assignments are scored by:
\begin{equation}
\text{Score} = 2 \cdot W_{\max} + W_{\text{total}} + 5000 \cdot \frac{\sum_k ||P_k| - A/N|}{A/N}
\label{eq:score}
\end{equation}
where $W_{\max}$ is the maximum walk distance (fairness, from Eq.~\ref{eq:walk}), $W_{\text{total}}$ is total walk distance across all ESWs (efficiency), and the third term penalizes address-count imbalance. The scoring weights fairness ($W_{\max}$) twice as heavily as efficiency ($W_{\text{total}}$).

\subsection{Oracle Algorithm (C5)}

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Oracle Assignment}
\label{alg:oracle}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Segments $\mathcal{S}$ with cost matrices, worker count $N$
\ENSURE Partition $\{P_1, \ldots, P_N\}$ minimizing $W_{\max}$
\STATE Precompute Dijkstra distances between all intersection nodes
\STATE $\text{best\_score} \leftarrow \infty$
\FOR{each strategy $\in$ \{NN, RH, Bisect\}}
    \IF{strategy $\in$ \{NN, RH\}}
        \FOR{each starting segment $s \in \mathcal{S}$}
            \STATE Build chain from $s$ using strategy's heuristic
            \STATE Slice chain into $N$ groups by address count
            \STATE Score partition (Eq.~\ref{eq:score})
            \STATE Update best if improved
        \ENDFOR
    \ELSE
        \STATE Recursively bisect $\mathcal{S}$ into $N$ geographic groups
        \STATE Order each group with NN chain
        \STATE Score partition (Eq.~\ref{eq:score})
        \STATE Update best if improved
    \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\RETURN best partition
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The oracle runs all strategies and all starting segments, then returns the partition with the lowest score. For 256 segments (Crescent Park), the oracle completes in under 1 second. The computational cost is dominated by the one-time Dijkstra precomputation (10.9\,s for 263 intersection nodes).

\section{Discussion}
%% ==================================================================

\subsection{Why the Right-Hand Rule Dominates}

With street-constrained distances, the cost of a geographically scattered partition is high: the ESW must walk along the road network between distant segments, accumulating unproductive distance. The right-hand rule's spiral pattern inherently produces partitions where consecutive segments are adjacent, minimizing transitions. Nearest-neighbor chaining can produce similar locality on small neighborhoods but tends to snake through larger ones. Bisect produces compact regions but axis-aligned cuts sometimes force transitions across the entire neighborhood to reach the next partition.

\subsection{The Unproductive Walking Bottleneck}

The 41.6\% average unproductive ratio reveals that nearly half of all walking produces no useful work. This finding has two implications:

\textbf{For algorithm design:} The dominant optimization target is transition reduction, not intra-segment traversal. The cost matrix already optimizes within-segment walking to near-optimality; further gains must come from smarter segment ordering.

\textbf{For practitioners:} When an NPC manually adjusts assignments, they should prioritize geographic contiguity (minimizing \textcolor{unprodred}{red} transitions) over address-count balance. A slightly unbalanced but geographically compact assignment outperforms a perfectly balanced but scattered one.

\subsection{The Cost Matrix Subsumes U-Turn}

In our earlier haversine-based study, the U-turn heuristic (walking one side of a street then returning on the other) was the single most impactful optimization, reducing intra-street walk distance by 33--71\%. In the segment-based formulation, this optimization is subsumed by the cost matrix. The matrix evaluates all traversal options per segment and selects the cheapest. In 97\% of segments, zigzag through-traversal is cheaper than U-turn because the street width ($\sim$10\,m) is less than the inter-address gap. The remaining 3\% (short, densely addressed segments) automatically receive U-turn routing.

\subsection{Practical Considerations}

The oracle algorithm runs in the browser (JavaScript) in under one second for neighborhoods up to 1,476 addresses (256 segments). The one-time Dijkstra precomputation adds 2--41 seconds depending on neighborhood size but needs to run only once per neighborhood.

The NPC retains the ability to manually adjust assignments after auto-generation. The auto-assign provides a strong starting point that the NPC can refine based on local knowledge (road closures, hazards, ESW locations).

\section{Related Work}
%% ==================================================================

The Capacitated Vehicle Routing Problem (CVRP)~\cite{toth2014} distributes service locations among vehicles. Our problem differs in having no depot, no capacity constraint, and a balance requirement. The Balanced Graph Partitioning problem~\cite{andreev2006} is closer but does not consider sequential ordering within partitions.

K-means clustering~\cite{lloyd1982} is widely used for geographic partitioning but does not guarantee balanced clusters. Balanced k-means variants~\cite{malinen2014} add cardinality constraints but increase complexity.

The nearest-neighbor heuristic for TSP~\cite{rosenkrantz1977} produces tours within $O(\log n)$ of optimal. Our variant operates on road-network segments with port-to-port costs.

Our within-segment traversal problem --- visiting points on two parallel sidewalks with a crossing penalty --- is related to the TSP on parallel lines~\cite{chung1988}. The cost matrix formulation precomputes optimal traversals, allowing the inter-segment algorithm to treat each segment as a black box.

Recursive bisection is used in computational geometry for balanced spatial decomposition~\cite{berger1987} and in VLSI placement~\cite{karypis1998}. Our application to address assignment on a road network appears to be novel.

\section{Conclusion}
%% ==================================================================

We presented a practical system for assigning addresses to emergency services workers that minimizes worst-case walking distance using road-network-constrained routing. We return to each stated contribution with the evidence supporting it:

\textbf{C1 (Segment model).} Decomposing the OpenStreetMap road network into 64,482 segments between 54,854 intersections, with 41,854 addresses snapped to 7,430 address-bearing segments, provides a physically accurate model of how ESWs walk.

\textbf{C2 (Cost matrix).} The $4 \times 4$ per-segment cost matrix captures zigzag, U-turn, through-walk, and out-and-back options in a unified formulation. In our data, 97\% of segments favor zigzag traversal; the 3\% that favor U-turn are identified automatically without a separate heuristic.

\textbf{C3 (Walking metric).} Decomposing walk distance into productive and unproductive components reveals that \textbf{41.6\% of walking is unproductive} (inter-segment transitions), identifying this as the dominant optimization target.

\textbf{C4 (Empirical evaluation).} Across 11 neighborhoods (54--1,737 addresses), the right-hand-rule chain wins 59\% of cases, achieving overhead ratios of 1.6--2.8$\times$ versus the theoretical ideal. No single algorithm dominates all geometries.

\textbf{C5 (Oracle algorithm).} Running all strategies and selecting the best by scoring completes in under one second for neighborhoods up to 1,476 addresses, enabling real-time use during emergency operations.

Future work includes accounting for ESW starting positions, dynamic reassignment as workers complete their areas, incorporating road-closure data, and exploring simulated annealing for post-optimization of segment ordering.
