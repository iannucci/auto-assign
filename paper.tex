\begin{abstract}
During disaster response, Emergency Services Workers (ESWs) must visit assigned addresses to assess damage. We study the problem of partitioning addresses across $N$ workers to minimize assessment completion time. We model the street network as road segments between intersections, each with a precomputed traversal cost matrix, and constrain all walking distances to follow streets. Three metrics capture what matters operationally: \emph{walk quality} (fraction of walking that visits addresses vs.\ transitions between segments), \emph{time spread} (difference between the first and last ESW to finish), and the \emph{productive-distance invariant} (the observation that productive walking is determined by the address set, not the algorithm --- only unproductive walking can be optimized). We evaluate nine algorithms --- nearest-neighbor chain, right-hand rule, 2-opt local search, recursive bisection, Voronoi with balanced seeds, BFS and DFS from fire station huddle points, simulated annealing, and an oracle --- across five Palo Alto neighborhoods chosen for maximum geometric diversity, at team sizes $N = 3, 5, 7, 10$. No single algorithm dominates: chain-based methods achieve the highest walk quality on compact neighborhoods (76\%), while Voronoi with balanced seeds wins on elongated layouts (74\%). All algorithms fail on sparse campuses ($<$30\% walk quality). The oracle reduces worst-case walking by 37\% vs.\ random assignment.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%% ==================================================================

Post-disaster damage assessment requires Emergency Services Workers (ESWs) to physically visit assigned addresses, inspect structures, and report conditions. The speed of this process directly affects emergency response effectiveness~\cite{fema2023}. In the ES-Link system~\cite{eslink}, a Neighborhood Preparedness Coordinator (NPC) assigns addresses to ESWs through a web-based map interface.

We study the automatic assignment problem: given a set of addresses on a road network and $N$ workers, find a partition that minimizes the time until all assessments are complete. The problem combines elements of the Capacitated Vehicle Routing Problem~\cite{toth2014} and balanced graph partitioning~\cite{andreev2006}, but differs in that our ``vehicles'' are on foot, start from a shared staging area, and must walk on streets --- they cannot jump between non-adjacent addresses.

Through development and evaluation of nine algorithms, we discover three insights that are not obvious from the problem statement:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Walk quality matters more than distance or time.} ESWs reject assignments with excessive purposeless walking regardless of what the time metric shows.
\item \textbf{Productive walking is invariant.} The distance spent visiting addresses is determined by the address set, not the algorithm. Only \emph{unproductive} walking (transitions between segments) can be optimized.
\item \textbf{Neighborhood geometry determines which algorithm wins.} No single method dominates across dense, grid, cul-de-sac, sparse, and elongated layouts.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:formulation}
%% ==================================================================

\subsection{Road Network Model}

We model the street network as a graph $G = (V, E)$ extracted from OpenStreetMap, where vertices $V$ are intersections (nodes shared by two or more road ways) and edges $E$ are \emph{segments} --- uninterrupted stretches of roadway between intersections. Within a segment, no turns are possible; turns occur only at endpoints.

Each address is snapped to its nearest segment by perpendicular projection (median snap distance 22.9\,m) and classified as left or right side by the cross product of the road direction vector and the address displacement vector.

\subsection{Segment Cost Matrix}

Each segment endpoint has two ports (one per sidewalk): $S_L, S_R, E_L, E_R$. We precompute a $4 \times 4$ cost matrix $\mathbf{C}$ giving the minimum walking distance for every (entry port, exit port) combination. Through-traversal costs $D + k \cdot w$ where $D$ is segment length, $w$ is street width, and $k$ counts the street crossings required by the address run pattern. Out-and-back traversal costs $2 \cdot t_n \cdot D + k \cdot w$ where $t_n$ is the farthest address position. The matrix automatically selects between zigzag (through-traversal with crossings) and U-turn (one side out, return on the other) based on the actual address geometry.

\subsection{Walking Distance}

The total walking distance for ESW $k$ is:
\begin{equation}
W_k = \underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^{m} C(\text{in}_i, \text{out}_i)}_{\displaystyle W_k^{\text{prod}}} + \underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^{m-1} d_{\text{road}}(\text{out}_i, \text{in}_{i+1})}_{\displaystyle W_k^{\text{unprod}}}
\label{eq:walk}
\end{equation}
where $C(\text{in}_i, \text{out}_i)$ is the cost of traversing segment $i$ (productive walking) and $d_{\text{road}}$ is the shortest-path distance along the road network between segments (unproductive walking).

\subsection{Data}

Segments are extracted from OpenStreetMap via \texttt{osm2pgsql} (Geofabrik California PBF, filtered to Palo Alto). From 35,912 road ways we build 64,482 segments; 7,430 have at least one snapped address from the City of Palo Alto municipal database (41,854 addresses). Road-network shortest paths are precomputed by Dijkstra within each neighborhood.

\section{Three Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}
%% ==================================================================

\subsection{Walk Quality}

Walk quality is the fraction of an ESW's total walking that is productive:
\begin{equation}
Q_k = \frac{W_k^{\text{prod}}}{W_k^{\text{prod}} + W_k^{\text{unprod}}}
\label{eq:wq}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Why it matters to individuals.} An ESW who walks past unvisited addresses to reach their assigned territory perceives the assignment as incompetent. Walk quality below 50\% means the ESW spends more time transitioning than assessing --- operationally unacceptable regardless of what the time metric shows.

\textbf{Why it matters to the response.} If ESWs lose confidence in their assignments, they may deviate from the prescribed route, creating coverage gaps. Walk quality is a proxy for assignment credibility.

\subsection{Time Spread}

The time spread is the difference between the first and last ESW to finish:
\begin{equation}
\text{Spread} = \max_k T_k - \min_k T_k, \qquad T_k = \frac{W_k}{v} + |P_k| \cdot t_{\text{assess}}
\label{eq:spread}
\end{equation}
where $v = 5$\,km/h is walking speed, $|P_k|$ is the address count, and $t_{\text{assess}} = 5$\,min is the per-address assessment time.

\textbf{Why it matters to individuals.} Fairness --- no ESW should work hours longer than another for the same event.

\textbf{Why it matters to the response.} The NPC cannot act on partial data. The straggler determines when relief is allocated. A 100-minute spread means the NPC waits 100 minutes after the fastest ESW finishes before having complete damage data.

\subsection{Productive-Distance Invariant}

The total productive walking distance $\sum_k W_k^{\text{prod}}$ is determined by the address set and the segment cost matrices --- it is \emph{invariant across algorithms}. All algorithms visit the same segments; the only difference is \emph{which order} and \emph{which ESW}. This means:

\begin{equation}
\sum_k W_k^{\text{prod}} \approx \text{constant for all algorithms}
\label{eq:invariant}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Significance.} Algorithms can only reduce unproductive walking, not productive walking. This reframes the optimization: instead of ``minimize total distance,'' the objective is ``minimize waste.'' Every comparison confirms this: the productive column in Table~\ref{tab:master} is constant within each neighborhood; the unproductive column tells the entire story.

\section{Algorithms}
\label{sec:algorithms}
%% ==================================================================

All algorithms operate on the segment graph: segments are nodes with four ports and precomputed cost matrices; edges carry road-network distances. We describe nine algorithms, each with its key idea. Pseudocode for the most important algorithms appears in Algorithm~\ref{alg:chainslice} and Algorithm~\ref{alg:oracle}.

\subsection{Chain+Slice (Nearest-Neighbor)}

Build a single-worker walk order using nearest-neighbor on ports: from the current exit port, select the unvisited segment minimizing transition + traversal cost. Partition the chain into $N$ groups at address-count boundaries $\lceil A/N \rceil$. Try all starting segments; keep the best.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Chain+Slice}
\label{alg:chainslice}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Segments $\mathcal{S}$ with cost matrices, worker count $N$
\STATE $\text{best} \leftarrow \infty$
\FOR{each starting segment $s_0 \in \mathcal{S}$}
    \STATE $\text{chain} \leftarrow [s_0]$; mark $s_0$ visited
    \WHILE{unvisited segments remain}
        \STATE $s^* \leftarrow \arg\min_{s \notin \text{chain}} [\text{transition}(\text{chain.last}, s) + C_s]$
        \STATE Append $s^*$ to chain
    \ENDWHILE
    \STATE Slice chain into $N$ groups at $\lceil A/N \rceil$ boundaries
    \STATE Score partition; update best if improved
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Right-Hand Rule Chain}

Variant of Chain+Slice that adds a clockwise turn-angle bias: $\text{score}(s) = d_{\text{road}} + 0.5 \cdot \theta_{\text{cw}} + 0.1 \cdot C_s$. This produces a spiral traversal pattern that keeps consecutive segments geographically adjacent.

\subsection{2-Opt Local Search}

Starting from the best NN chain, iteratively reverse sub-chains if the reversal reduces total walk distance. Each reversed segment swaps its entry/exit ports. Converges in $O(M^3)$ for $M$ segments.

\subsection{Recursive Bisection}

Split segments by centroid along the longer geographic axis at the address-count midpoint. Recurse until $N$ groups remain. Order each group with NN chain.

\subsection{Voronoi with Balanced Seeds}

Assign segments to the nearest of $N$ seed points by Euclidean distance. Segments straddling a cell boundary (endpoints in different cells) are reassigned wholly to one cell. Standard Lloyd's iteration moves seeds to address-weighted centroids but minimizes variance, not balance. We replace it with greedy hill-climbing: starting from Lloyd's result, move each seed to random road nodes, keeping any move that reduces address-count imbalance. For small neighborhoods ($\leq 41$ nodes, $N = 3$), we use exhaustive search. Within each cell, segments are ordered by NN chain.

\subsection{BFS from Huddle}

All $N$ ESWs start at the fire station fan-out point. Round-robin: each round, assign each ESW the unvisited segment with the smallest marginal time increase from their current frontier.

\subsection{DFS from Huddle}

Build one ESW's assignment at a time. Greedily add nearest segments until estimated time reaches $T^* = (\text{total time}) / N$. Move to next ESW. The last ESW takes all remaining segments.

\subsection{SA Post-Processing}

Starting from BFS's result, transfer boundary segments from the heaviest ESW to the lightest, accepting worse solutions with Boltzmann probability to escape local optima.

\subsection{Oracle}

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Oracle}
\label{alg:oracle}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Segments $\mathcal{S}$, worker count $N$
\STATE Run Chain+Slice, Right-Hand, 2-Opt, Bisect
\STATE Score each by $2 \cdot W_{\max} + W_{\text{total}} + 5000 \cdot \text{imbalance}$
\RETURN partition with lowest score
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Random Baseline}

Shuffle segments, partition by address count, NN chain within each group. Run 30 trials, keep best. Quantifies the value of optimization.

\section{Simulation Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
%% ==================================================================

\textbf{Data pipeline.} OpenStreetMap data is imported via \texttt{osm2pgsql} from a Geofabrik California PBF (1.3\,GB), filtered to the Palo Alto bounding box. Road segments are split at intersection nodes. Municipal addresses are snapped to segments by perpendicular projection. Cost matrices are precomputed from address positions and street widths (estimated from OSM road class).

\textbf{Distance computation.} Dijkstra shortest paths are precomputed between all intersection nodes within each neighborhood (2--31\,s depending on size). All inter-segment distances use road-network paths, not haversine.

\textbf{Parameters.} $t_{\text{assess}} = 5$\,min/address, walking speed $v = 5$\,km/h. Huddle point = nearest intersection node to the serving fire station (6 Palo Alto Fire Department stations, assigned by centroid proximity).

\textbf{Test matrix.} 10 algorithms $\times$ 5 neighborhoods $\times$ $N \in \{3, 5, 7, 10\}$ = 200 test cases. Each evaluated with all three metrics. All algorithms use the same segment data, distance matrices, and evaluation function.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% ==================================================================

\subsection{When Each Algorithm Wins}

The five diverse neighborhoods partition the algorithm landscape:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Dense, compact (PAC):} Chain methods dominate (76\% WQ). All segments are adjacent; transitions are inherently short. Voronoi and Bisect struggle because any partition boundary creates unnecessary crossings in this small cluster.
\item \textbf{Cul-de-sacs (FM):} 2-opt wins (73\%) by refining chain ordering to follow the radial topology. Voronoi is competitive at large $N$ (80\% at $N = 10$) as cells align with cul-de-sac clusters.
\item \textbf{Regular grid (CC):} All methods perform similarly (61--71\% WQ). The grid aligns with both chain ordering and Voronoi boundaries. 2-opt's local search provides a small edge.
\item \textbf{Sparse campus (RP):} Everything fails ($<$30\% WQ). The fundamental problem is not the algorithm but the data: 289 segments with only 584 addresses means 2.0 addresses per segment --- most walking is between segments with no addresses.
\item \textbf{Elongated (SG):} Voronoi wins decisively (74\% vs.\ 52\% for 2-opt). The Euclidean partition naturally bisects the elongated shape. Chain methods snake through the length, producing interleaved partitions.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Productive-Distance Invariant}

Table~\ref{tab:master} confirms that productive distance is invariant ($\pm$5\%) across all algorithms within each neighborhood. This is the paper's deepest insight: \textbf{algorithms cannot reduce work --- they can only reduce waste.} The practical implication: when evaluating a new algorithm, compare its \emph{unproductive} distance to the best known, not its total distance.

\subsection{Recommended Practice}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Transport ESWs to the neighborhood.} Walking from the fire station dominates walk quality when the station is distant.
\item \textbf{Run the oracle at the fan-out point.} Completes in $<$1\,s for neighborhoods up to 289 segments.
\item \textbf{Check time spread.} If $>$30\,min, manually transfer boundary segments.
\item \textbf{Brief ESWs with trail maps.} Show productive (blue) and unproductive (red) walking. ESWs who see their route is efficient work with confidence.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}

\textbf{Single city.} All neighborhoods are in Palo Alto. Results may differ in dense urban, rural, or irregular environments. \textbf{No field validation.} ESWs have not been observed using these assignments. \textbf{Flat terrain.} Walking speed is assumed constant. \textbf{Equal service time.} All addresses treated equally.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ==================================================================

The Capacitated Vehicle Routing Problem~\cite{toth2014} distributes locations among vehicles from a depot. Our problem has no depot and requires balance. Postal delivery optimization~\cite{eiselt1995} is the closest analogue: letter carriers walk segments, balancing workload and minimizing deadheading. Census enumeration area delineation~\cite{taylor2012} partitions addresses into balanced geographic groups. K-means~\cite{lloyd1982} and balanced variants~\cite{malinen2014} cluster geographically but ignore street connectivity. The nearest-neighbor TSP heuristic~\cite{rosenkrantz1977} produces $O(\log n)$-competitive tours. Our within-segment problem relates to the TSP on parallel lines~\cite{frederickson1978}. Recursive bisection~\cite{berger1987, karypis1998} is standard for spatial decomposition. Simulated annealing~\cite{kirkpatrick1983} provides the post-optimization framework.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ==================================================================

We studied the problem of assigning addresses to emergency services workers on a road network. Three metrics --- walk quality, time spread, and the productive-distance invariant --- capture what matters operationally. Nine algorithms were evaluated across five geometrically diverse neighborhoods at team sizes 3--10.

The central finding is that \textbf{no single algorithm dominates}. Chain-based methods (2-opt, nearest-neighbor) achieve the highest walk quality on compact and grid neighborhoods (71--76\%). Voronoi with balanced seeds wins on elongated layouts (74\%). All algorithms fail on sparse campuses ($<$30\%). The oracle --- running all methods and selecting the best --- reduces worst-case walking by 37\% vs.\ random assignment.

The productive-distance invariant reveals that algorithms can only reduce waste, not work. This reframes future research: the objective is not ``better routing'' but ``less unproductive walking.'' Promising directions include Voronoi + chain+slice hybrids (Voronoi for geographic partitioning, chain+slice for intra-cell routing), constraint-solver formulations that explicitly balance walk quality and time spread, and field validation with ESW teams.
