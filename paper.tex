\begin{abstract}
During disaster response, Emergency Services Workers (ESWs) must visit assigned addresses to assess damage. We study the problem of partitioning addresses across $N$ workers to minimize assessment completion time. We model the street network as road segments between intersections, each with a precomputed traversal cost matrix, and constrain all walking distances to follow streets. Three metrics capture what matters operationally: \emph{walk quality} (fraction of walking that visits addresses vs.\ transitions between segments), \emph{time spread} (difference between the first and last ESW to finish), and the \emph{productive-distance invariant} (the observation that productive walking is determined by the address set, not the algorithm --- only unproductive walking can be optimized). We evaluate nine algorithms --- nearest-neighbor chain, right-hand rule, 2-opt local search, recursive bisection, Voronoi with balanced seeds, BFS and DFS from fire station huddle points, simulated annealing, and an oracle --- across five Palo Alto neighborhoods chosen for maximum geometric diversity, at team sizes $N = 3, 5, 7, 10$. No single algorithm dominates: chain-based methods achieve the highest walk quality on compact neighborhoods (76\%), while Voronoi with balanced seeds wins on elongated layouts (74\%). All algorithms fail on sparse campuses ($<$30\% walk quality). The oracle reduces worst-case walking by 37\% vs.\ random assignment.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%% ==================================================================

Post-disaster damage assessment requires Emergency Services Workers (ESWs) to physically visit assigned addresses, inspect structures, and report conditions. The speed of this process directly affects emergency response effectiveness~\cite{fema2023}. In the ES-Link system~\cite{eslink}, a Neighborhood Preparedness Coordinator (NPC) assigns addresses to ESWs through a web-based map interface. Manual assignment is tedious and does not optimize for worker travel distance.

We study the automatic assignment problem: given a set of addresses on a road network and $N$ workers, find a partition that minimizes the time until all assessments are complete. The problem combines elements of the Capacitated Vehicle Routing Problem (CVRP)~\cite{toth2014} and balanced graph partitioning~\cite{andreev2006}, but differs in that our ``vehicles'' are on foot, start from a shared staging area, and must walk on streets --- they cannot jump between non-adjacent addresses. The general problem is NP-hard~\cite{garey1979}, so we seek practical heuristics that produce good solutions in real-time.

Through development and evaluation of nine algorithms, we discover three insights that are not obvious from the problem statement:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Walk quality matters more than distance or time.} ESWs reject assignments with excessive purposeless walking regardless of what the time metric shows.
\item \textbf{Productive walking is invariant.} The distance spent visiting addresses is determined by the address set, not the algorithm. Only \emph{unproductive} walking (transitions between segments) can be optimized.
\item \textbf{Neighborhood geometry determines which algorithm wins.} No single method dominates across dense, grid, cul-de-sac, sparse, and elongated layouts.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
\item[\textbf{C1.}] A road-network segment model with a geometric pipeline for address snapping and side-of-street classification (Section~\ref{sec:formulation}).
\item[\textbf{C2.}] A per-segment cost matrix that captures all traversal options as an emergent property of address geometry (Section~\ref{sec:costmatrix}).
\item[\textbf{C3.}] Three walking metrics --- walk quality, time spread, and the productive-distance invariant --- that capture what matters operationally (Section~\ref{sec:metrics}).
\item[\textbf{C4.}] Empirical evaluation across five diverse neighborhoods at $N = 3, 5, 7, 10$ showing the winning strategy depends on neighborhood geometry (Section~\ref{sec:results}).
\item[\textbf{C5.}] An oracle algorithm that runs all strategies and selects the best, reducing worst-case walk by 37\% vs.\ random assignment, in under one second (Section~\ref{sec:oracle}).
\item[\textbf{C6.}] A time-based reformulation using fire station huddle points showing that walking quality must be a hard constraint, not just a cost component (Section~\ref{sec:time-results}).
\item[\textbf{C7.}] Voronoi partitioning with balanced seed search achieves competitive results (51\% walk quality, 99\,min spread), overturning the naive Lloyd's negative result (Section~\ref{sec:voronoi-results}).
\item[\textbf{C8.}] A constrained formulation revealing that walkable contiguity, time balance, and walking quality are in fundamental tension --- the resolution depends on operational context (Section~\ref{sec:constraint-results}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:formulation}
%% ==================================================================

\subsection{Road Network Model (C1)}

We model the street network as a graph $G = (V, E)$ extracted from OpenStreetMap (OSM), where vertices $V$ are \emph{intersections} and edges $E$ are \emph{segments}.

\textbf{Intersection:} A node shared by two or more OSM ways, or a way endpoint. We identify intersections by counting way membership: any node appearing in more than one way, plus all way endpoints, is an intersection.

\textbf{Segment:} A maximal chain of road edges between two intersection nodes, with street name, road class, polyline geometry, and length $D$ (meters). Within a segment, no turns are possible; turns occur only at endpoints. Each segment endpoint has two \emph{ports} (one per sidewalk): $S_L, S_R$ at the start intersection and $E_L, E_R$ at the end.

\subsubsection{Address Snapping}

Each address has a geographic coordinate (latitude, longitude) from the municipal database. We snap it to the road network by finding the nearest point on any segment polyline:

\begin{enumerate}
\item For each edge $(v_i, v_{i+1})$ of each segment's polyline, compute the point-to-line-segment distance using perpendicular projection, clamped to $[0,1]$.
\item Select the segment and edge with minimum distance. Reject addresses $> 200$\,m from any segment (only 3 of 41,857 addresses).
\item Compute the fractional position $t \in [0,1]$ along the segment by accumulating edge lengths up to the projection point.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Side-of-Street Classification}

Each snapped address is classified as \emph{left} or \emph{right} of the segment centerline using the cross product of the road direction vector $\vec{d} = (v_{i+1} - v_i)$ and the address displacement vector $\vec{a} = (p - v_i)$:

\begin{equation}
\text{side} = \begin{cases} \text{left} & \text{if } \vec{d} \times \vec{a} \geq 0 \\ \text{right} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
\label{eq:side}
\end{equation}

This geometric classification is more reliable than house-number parity (even/odd), which can be inconsistent across jurisdictions.

\subsubsection{Data Quality}

From 41,857 municipal addresses within the Palo Alto bounding box, 41,854 snap successfully to one of 7,430 address-bearing segments. Snap distances have median 22.9\,m (consistent with the typical setback of houses from the road centerline), 87\% are within 30\,m, and only 1\% exceed 50\,m. Segment lengths have median 63\,m, mean 83\,m, and range from 0.5\,m to 2,288\,m. Addresses per segment: 31\% have one address, 39\% have 2--5, 15\% have 6--10, and 15\% have 11 or more (up to 135).

\subsection{Segment Cost Matrix (C2)}
\label{sec:costmatrix}

The cost matrix $\mathbf{C}$ is a $4 \times 4$ matrix giving the minimum walking distance for every (entry port, exit port) combination.

\textbf{Definitions.} Let $D$ be the segment length, $w$ the street width (estimated from OSM road class: residential $\approx 10$\,m, service $\approx 6$\,m, secondary $\approx 14$\,m), $n$ the address count, and $r$ the number of \emph{runs} --- maximal consecutive groups of addresses on the same side when sorted by fractional position $t$.

\textbf{Through-traversal} (enter at $S$, exit at $E$ or vice versa). The ESW walks monotonically along the segment, crossing the street at each run boundary. The cost is:
\begin{equation}
C(S_s, E_e) = D + k(s,e) \cdot w
\label{eq:through}
\end{equation}
where $k(s,e) = (r{-}1) + [s \neq s_1] + [e \neq s_r]$ counts street crossings: $(r{-}1)$ mandatory crossings at run boundaries, plus one if the entry side $s$ differs from the first run's side $s_1$, plus one if the exit side $e$ differs from the last run's side $s_r$. The notation $[\cdot]$ is the Iverson bracket (1 if true, 0 if false).

\textbf{Out-and-back} (enter and exit at the same end). The ESW walks to the farthest address and returns. The return carries no unvisited addresses, so incurs zero crossings:
\begin{equation}
C(S_s, S_e) = 2 \cdot t_n \cdot D + k_{\text{out}}(s) \cdot w
\label{eq:outback}
\end{equation}
where $t_n$ is the position of the last address. The exit side $e$ is free (the ESW returns on whichever sidewalk is convenient).

\textbf{Monotonicity.} Within a segment, the ESW never backtracks. Backtracking costs $2g$ (twice the gap) while crossing costs $w$; since residential street widths ($w \approx 10$\,m) are less than typical inter-address gaps ($g \approx 15$--$30$\,m), crossing always dominates.

\subsubsection{Worked Example}
\label{sec:example}

Consider a 150\,m residential segment ($w = 10$\,m) with 8 addresses at positions and sides:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ccccccccc}
$t$:  & 0.10 & 0.20 & 0.30 & 0.45 & 0.55 & 0.70 & 0.80 & 0.90 \\
side: & L  & L  & L  & R  & R  & L  & L  & R
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The runs are: LLL (3 left), RR (2 right), LL (2 left), R (1 right) --- so $r = 4$ with $s_1 = L$, $s_r = R$. Mandatory crossings: $r - 1 = 3$.

\textbf{Through-traversal $S_L \to E_R$:} $k = 3 + [L \neq L] + [R \neq R] = 3$. Cost $= 150 + 3 \times 10 = 180$\,m.

\textbf{Through-traversal $S_R \to E_R$:} $k = 3 + [R \neq L] + [R \neq R] = 4$. Cost $= 150 + 40 = 190$\,m.

\textbf{Out-and-back $S_L \to S_L$:} $t_n = 0.90$, $k_{\text{out}} = 3$. Cost $= 2 \times 0.90 \times 150 + 30 = 300$\,m. Much more expensive because the ESW backtracks 135\,m.

The full $4 \times 4$ matrix (rows = entry, columns = exit, in meters):

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{l|cccc}
    & $S_L$ & $S_R$ & $E_L$ & $E_R$ \\
\hline
$S_L$  & 300  & 300  & 190  & \textbf{180} \\
$S_R$  & 310  & 310  & 200  & 190 \\
$E_L$  & 190  & 200  & 314  & 314 \\
$E_R$  & \textbf{180}  & 190  & 304  & 304
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The cheapest option is $S_L \to E_R$ (or equivalently $E_R \to S_L$) at 180\,m: enter on the left, cross three times as runs alternate, exit on the right. The algorithm never computes this matrix at runtime --- it is precomputed in $O(n)$ time from the sorted address sequence.

\subsubsection{Zigzag vs.\ U-Turn}

The matrix automatically selects between zigzag (through-traversal with crossings) and U-turn (one side out, cross, other side back). In our dataset of 3,663 segments with addresses on both sides:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{97\% prefer zigzag} (3,549 segments). These have average length 103\,m, average 5.4 runs, and 8.3 addresses. The crossings ($\sim$4 $\times$ 10\,m $= 40$\,m) are much less than the backtrack penalty ($\sim$2 $\times$ 103\,m $= 206$\,m).
\item \textbf{3\% prefer U-turn} (114 segments). These are shorter (average 75\,m), denser (average 19.1 addresses), with more runs (average 11.1). The crossing cost ($\sim$10 $\times$ 10\,m $= 100$\,m) exceeds the short backtrack ($\sim$2 $\times$ 75\,m $= 150$\,m), tipping the balance.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Walking Distance}

The total walking distance for ESW $k$, assigned an ordered sequence of segments $P_k = (seg_1, seg_2, \ldots, seg_m)$ with chosen ports, is:

\begin{equation}
W_k = \underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^{m} C(\text{in}_i, \text{out}_i)}_{\displaystyle W_k^{\text{prod}}} + \underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^{m-1} d_{\text{road}}(\text{out}_i, \text{in}_{i+1})}_{\displaystyle W_k^{\text{unprod}}}
\label{eq:walk}
\end{equation}

\begin{itemize}
\item $C(\text{in}_i, \text{out}_i)$ is the cost of traversing segment $i$, read from its precomputed cost matrix. This is \textbf{productive walking}: every meter spent visiting addresses.
\item $d_{\text{road}}(\text{out}_i, \text{in}_{i+1})$ is the shortest-path distance along the road network from the exit port of segment $i$ to the entry port of segment $i{+}1$, computed by Dijkstra's algorithm on the intersection graph. This is \textbf{unproductive walking}: meters spent traveling between segments without visiting any addresses.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Theoretical ideal.} If addresses could be partitioned with zero unproductive walking and perfect balance:
\begin{equation}
W^* = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{\text{all segments}} \min_{\text{in}, \text{out}} C(\text{in}, \text{out})
\label{eq:ideal}
\end{equation}
This is unachievable because: (1) geographic partitioning creates boundaries requiring inter-segment transitions, and (2) integer segment counts prevent exact balance. The \textbf{overhead ratio} $\rho = W_{\max} / W^*$ measures how close the assignment comes. $\rho = 1.0$ is perfect; we observe $\rho$ between 1.6 and 2.8.

\textbf{Objective.} Minimize the worst-case walking distance:
\begin{equation}
\min_{\{P_1,\ldots,P_N\}} \max_{k} W_k \qquad \text{s.t.} \quad \frac{\max_k |P_k|}{\min_k |P_k|} \leq \beta
\label{eq:objective}
\end{equation}
where $|P_k|$ is the address count in partition $k$ and $\beta \approx 1.0$ is the balance tolerance.

\subsection{General Constraints}

The following constraints apply to all algorithms and evaluations:

\textbf{Whole-segment assignment.} Each segment is assigned to exactly one ESW. No segment is split between workers. This is a hard constraint: an ESW visiting half a street while another visits the other half is operationally confusing and creates boundary disputes.

\textbf{Shared starting location (huddle point).} All ESWs begin at a shared staging area --- the neighborhood intersection nearest to the serving fire station. The walk from huddle to each ESW's first segment is included in their total walking distance and time. We assume ESWs are transported to the huddle by vehicle; the walk from fire station to huddle is not counted.

\textbf{Street-constrained walking.} ESWs must follow the road network at all times. They may walk along a segment, turn at an intersection, or cross a street, but may not jump over houses or cut through properties. All inter-segment distances use Dijkstra shortest paths on the road network.

\textbf{Assessment time.} Each address requires a fixed assessment time $t_{\text{assess}} = 5$\,min. The finish time for ESW $k$ is:
\begin{equation}
T_k = \frac{W_k}{v} + |P_k| \cdot t_{\text{assess}}
\label{eq:finish-time}
\end{equation}
where $v = 5$\,km/h is walking speed and $|P_k|$ is the address count. Assessment time typically dominates walking time by $\sim$8$\times$.

\subsection{Data}

Segments are extracted from OpenStreetMap via \texttt{osm2pgsql} (Geofabrik California PBF, 1.3\,GB) filtered to the Palo Alto bounding box ($37.35$--$37.48^\circ$N, $122.05$--$122.20^\circ$W). From 35,912 OSM road ways and 146,957 nodes, we identify 54,854 intersections and build 64,482 segments. Of these, 7,430 have at least one snapped address. Road-network shortest paths are precomputed by Dijkstra within each neighborhood (2--14\,s depending on size). Huddle points use 6 Palo Alto Fire Department stations, assigned to neighborhoods by centroid proximity.

Inter-segment transitions --- the \emph{unproductive} component of Eq.~\ref{eq:walk} --- are computed as shortest paths on the road network graph, not as straight-line distances. \section{Three Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}
%% ==================================================================

\subsection{Walk Quality}

Walk quality is the fraction of an ESW's total walking that is productive:
\begin{equation}
Q_k = \frac{W_k^{\text{prod}}}{W_k^{\text{prod}} + W_k^{\text{unprod}}}
\label{eq:wq}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Why it matters to individuals.} An ESW who walks past unvisited addresses to reach their assigned territory perceives the assignment as incompetent. Walk quality below 50\% means the ESW spends more time transitioning than assessing --- operationally unacceptable regardless of what the time metric shows.

\textbf{Why it matters to the response.} If ESWs lose confidence in their assignments, they may deviate from the prescribed route, creating coverage gaps. Walk quality is a proxy for assignment credibility.

\subsection{Time Spread}

The time spread is the difference between the first and last ESW to finish:
\begin{equation}
\text{Spread} = \max_k T_k - \min_k T_k, \qquad T_k = \frac{W_k}{v} + |P_k| \cdot t_{\text{assess}}
\label{eq:spread}
\end{equation}
where $v = 5$\,km/h is walking speed, $|P_k|$ is the address count, and $t_{\text{assess}} = 5$\,min is the per-address assessment time.

\textbf{Why it matters to individuals.} Fairness --- no ESW should work hours longer than another for the same event.

\textbf{Why it matters to the response.} The NPC cannot act on partial data. The straggler determines when relief is allocated. A 100-minute spread means the NPC waits 100 minutes after the fastest ESW finishes before having complete damage data.

\subsection{Productive-Distance Invariant}

The total productive walking distance $\sum_k W_k^{\text{prod}}$ is determined by the address set and the segment cost matrices --- it is \emph{invariant across algorithms}. All algorithms visit the same segments; the only difference is \emph{which order} and \emph{which ESW}. This means:

\begin{equation}
\sum_k W_k^{\text{prod}} \approx \text{constant for all algorithms}
\label{eq:invariant}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Significance.} Algorithms can only reduce unproductive walking, not productive walking. This reframes the optimization: instead of ``minimize total distance,'' the objective is ``minimize waste.'' Every comparison confirms this: the productive column in Table~\ref{tab:master} is constant within each neighborhood; the unproductive column tells the entire story.

\textbf{Palo Alto Central (PAC).} 145 addresses packed into 11 segments at 9,686 addresses/km$^2$. Nearly all segments are adjacent --- transitions are short. The challenge is partitioning a single dense cluster.

\textbf{Fairmeadow (FM).} 307 addresses across 43 segments in a cul-de-sac layout. Streets radiate from a central spine. Algorithms that follow the cul-de-sac topology excel; those that cut across it fragment walking paths.

\textbf{Community Center (CC).} 489 addresses on 117 segments in a regular grid (71\% of streets within 25$^\circ$ of cardinal directions). All algorithms perform reasonably because the grid aligns with both chain ordering and Voronoi boundaries.

\textbf{Research Park (RP).} 584 addresses spread across 289 segments at only 70 addresses/km$^2$. The sparse campus layout means most walking is unproductive regardless of algorithm. This is the ``universal failure case.''

\textbf{Southgate (SG).} 268 addresses on 44 segments in an elongated layout (aspect ratio 1.9). The single dominant axis aligns with Euclidean Voronoi boundaries, making this the neighborhood where Voronoi wins.

\section{Algorithms}
\label{sec:algorithms}
%% ==================================================================

All algorithms operate on the segment graph: segments are nodes with four ports and precomputed cost matrices; edges carry road-network distances between shared intersections. We describe nine algorithms below, each with pseudocode and a Fairmeadow $N=3$ map.

\subsection{Chain+Slice (Nearest-Neighbor)}

Build a single-worker walk order using nearest-neighbor on ports: from the current exit port, select the unvisited segment minimizing transition + traversal cost. Partition the chain into $N$ groups at address-count boundaries $\lceil A/N \rceil$. Try all starting segments; keep the best.

\textbf{Complexity:} $O(M^2 \cdot 16)$ per starting segment (for each of $M$ segments, evaluate 16 port pairs against all remaining), times $M$ starting segments. Total: $O(M^3)$, mitigated by the precomputed distance matrix which makes each port-pair lookup $O(1)$.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Chain+Slice}
\label{alg:chainslice}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Segments $\mathcal{S}$ with cost matrices, worker count $N$
\STATE $\text{best} \leftarrow \infty$
\FOR{each starting segment $s_0 \in \mathcal{S}$}
  \STATE $\text{chain} \leftarrow [s_0]$; mark $s_0$ visited
  \WHILE{unvisited segments remain}
    \STATE $s^* \leftarrow \arg\min_{s \notin \text{chain}} [\text{transition}(\text{chain.last}, s) + C_s]$
    \STATE Append $s^*$ to chain
  \ENDWHILE
  \STATE Slice chain into $N$ groups at $\lceil A/N \rceil$ boundaries
  \STATE Score partition; update best if improved
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Right-Hand Rule Chain}

A variant of Chain+Slice that adds a directional bias. After exiting a segment, candidates are scored by:
\begin{equation}
\text{score}(s) = d_{\text{road}}(\text{exit}, \text{entry}_s) + 0.5 \cdot \theta_{\text{cw}} + 0.1 \cdot C_s
\label{eq:rh-score}
\end{equation}
where $d_{\text{road}}$ is the road-network transition distance, $\theta_{\text{cw}} \in [0^\circ, 360^\circ)$ is the clockwise turn angle from the current heading to the candidate entry point, and $C_s$ is the candidate's traversal cost. The 0.5 weight on turn angle (in degrees, so $0.5 \times 180^\circ = 90$\,m equivalent for a U-turn) biases toward continuing in roughly the same direction, producing a spiral traversal pattern. The 0.1 weight on segment cost is a tiebreaker favoring cheaper segments.

\textbf{Complexity:} Same as NN, $O(M^3)$.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Right-Hand Rule Chain}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Segments $\mathcal{S}$, worker count $N$, current heading $\vec{h}$
\STATE $\text{best} \leftarrow \infty$
\FOR{each starting segment $s_0 \in \mathcal{S}$}
    \STATE $\text{chain} \leftarrow [s_0]$; $\vec{h} \leftarrow$ direction of $s_0$
    \WHILE{unvisited segments remain}
        \STATE $s^* \leftarrow \arg\min_{s} [d_{\text{road}} + 0.5 \cdot \theta_{\text{cw}}(\vec{h}, s) + 0.1 \cdot C_s]$
        \STATE Append $s^*$; $\vec{h} \leftarrow$ exit direction of $s^*$
    \ENDWHILE
    \STATE Slice into $N$ groups; score; update best
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{2-Opt Local Search}

Starting from the best NN chain, iteratively improve segment ordering by reversing sub-chains. For each pair $(i, j)$ in the chain, reverse the sub-chain $[i..j]$ if the reversal reduces total walk distance. When a sub-chain is reversed, each segment's traversal direction flips: the entry and exit ports swap, and the segment cost is re-read from the cost matrix with transposed indices. Transition costs between the reversed segments and their neighbors are recomputed via the road-network distance matrix. Iterate until no improving swap exists (or 500 passes).

\textbf{Why 2-opt helps:} The NN chain is greedy --- it selects the next segment by minimizing immediate cost without considering downstream consequences. 2-opt repairs these local mistakes by finding pairs of transitions that form a ``cross'' (where reversing the intermediate sub-chain ``uncrosses'' them, reducing total distance). On our data, 2-opt reduces worst-case walk by 3.2\% on average and up to 41.3\% in the best case.

\textbf{Complexity:} $O(M^2)$ per pass, typically converging in $O(M)$ passes, giving $O(M^3)$ total.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{2-Opt Local Search}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Chain from best Chain+Slice run
\REPEAT
    \STATE $\text{improved} \leftarrow \text{false}$
    \FOR{each pair $(i, j)$ in chain}
        \STATE Reverse sub-chain $[i..j]$; swap entry/exit ports
        \STATE Recompute transition costs at boundaries
        \IF{total walk distance decreased}
            \STATE Accept reversal; $\text{improved} \leftarrow \text{true}$
        \ELSE
            \STATE Undo reversal
        \ENDIF
    \ENDFOR
\UNTIL{$\neg\text{improved}$ or 500 passes}
\STATE Slice into $N$ groups; score
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Recursive Bisection}

Split segments by centroid along the longer geographic axis (latitude or longitude) at the address-count midpoint. Recurse until $N$ groups remain. Within each group, build an NN chain to order segments. Produces compact rectangular regions with good geographic contiguity, but axis-aligned cuts may not follow street patterns.

\textbf{Complexity:} $O(M \log N)$ for splitting, plus $O((M/N)^3)$ for NN within each group.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Recursive Bisection}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Segments $\mathcal{S}$, worker count $N$
\STATE $\text{groups} \leftarrow [\mathcal{S}]$
\WHILE{$|\text{groups}| < N$}
    \STATE Select largest group $G$
    \STATE $\text{axis} \leftarrow$ longer of latitude, longitude span of $G$
    \STATE Sort $G$ by centroid along axis
    \STATE Split $G$ at address-count midpoint into $G_1, G_2$
    \STATE Replace $G$ with $G_1, G_2$ in groups
\ENDWHILE
\FOR{each group $G_k$}
    \STATE Order segments in $G_k$ by NN chain
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Voronoi with Balanced Seeds}

Assign segments to the nearest of $N$ seed points by Euclidean distance (consistent with straight-line Voronoi partition boundaries). Segments straddling a cell boundary (endpoints in different cells) are reassigned wholly to the closer cell.

\textbf{Seed optimization.} Standard Lloyd's iteration~\cite{lloyd1982} minimizes within-cell variance, not address count balance --- it converges to unbalanced cells (Fig.~\ref{fig:voronoi}, left). We replace it with greedy hill-climbing: starting from Lloyd's result, try moving each seed to random road nodes (50 trials per seed, 10 restarts), keeping any move that reduces imbalance. For small neighborhoods ($\leq 41$ nodes, $N = 3$), we use exhaustive search over all $\binom{|V|}{N}$ seed combinations (Fig.~\ref{fig:voronoi}, right). Within each cell, segments are ordered by NN chain.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Voronoi with Balanced Seeds}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Segments $\mathcal{S}$, worker count $N$, road nodes $V$
\STATE Initialize seeds via Lloyd's iteration (address-weighted centroids)
\FOR{10 random restarts}
    \FOR{each seed $k = 1 \ldots N$}
        \FOR{50 random trials}
            \STATE Move seed $k$ to random node $v \in V$
            \STATE Assign segments to nearest seed (Euclidean)
            \IF{address-count imbalance decreased}
                \STATE Keep new seed position
            \ENDIF
        \ENDFOR
    \ENDFOR
\ENDFOR
\STATE Reassign straddling segments wholly to closer cell
\STATE Order each cell's segments by NN chain
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{BFS from Huddle}

Starting from the huddle point (Section~\ref{sec:formulation}), assign segments round-robin: each round, for each ESW $k$, assign the unvisited segment minimizing marginal time increase:
\begin{equation}
\Delta T_k = \frac{d_{\text{road}}(\text{frontier}_k, \text{entry}_s) + C(\text{in}_s, \text{out}_s)}{v} + n_s \cdot t_{\text{assess}}
\label{eq:marginal}
\end{equation}
where $\text{frontier}_k$ is ESW $k$'s current exit port and $n_s$ is the segment's address count. Produces ``pizza slice'' partitions radiating from the huddle, but these interleave geographically, doubling unproductive walking vs.\ chain methods.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{BFS from Huddle}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Segments $\mathcal{S}$, worker count $N$, huddle point $H$
\STATE $\text{frontier}_k \leftarrow H$ for all $k = 1 \ldots N$
\WHILE{unassigned segments remain}
    \FOR{$k = 1$ to $N$}
        \STATE $s^* \leftarrow \arg\min_s \Delta T_k(s)$ \COMMENT{Eq.~\ref{eq:marginal}}
        \STATE Assign $s^*$ to ESW $k$; update $\text{frontier}_k$
    \ENDFOR
\ENDWHILE
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{DFS from Huddle}

Build one ESW's assignment at a time. Starting from the huddle point, compute a target finish time $T^* = (\text{total walk time} + A \cdot t_{\text{assess}}) / N$. For ESW 1, greedily add nearest segments until $T_1 \approx T^*$. Repeat for ESW 2 from the huddle with remaining segments. The last ESW takes all remaining segments. Suffers from the ``dregs problem'': the last ESW inherits scattered remnants, producing time spreads of 100--500\,min.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{DFS from Huddle}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Segments $\mathcal{S}$, worker count $N$, huddle $H$
\STATE $T^* \leftarrow (\text{total walk} + A \cdot t_{\text{assess}}) / N$
\FOR{$k = 1$ to $N - 1$}
    \STATE $\text{frontier} \leftarrow H$; $T_k \leftarrow 0$
    \WHILE{$T_k < T^*$ and unassigned segments remain}
        \STATE $s^* \leftarrow$ nearest unassigned segment to frontier
        \STATE Assign $s^*$ to ESW $k$; update frontier and $T_k$
    \ENDWHILE
\ENDFOR
\STATE Assign all remaining segments to ESW $N$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{SA Post-Processing}

Starting from BFS's result, apply simulated annealing~\cite{kirkpatrick1983}: transfer boundary segments from the heaviest ESW to the lightest, accepting worse solutions with Boltzmann probability $e^{-\Delta/T}$ to escape local optima. Temperature cools from $T_0 = 0.5 \cdot \text{spread}$ to $T_f = 0.01$ over 500 iterations. The key mechanism: a two-move backtracking sequence can achieve gains that no single greedy transfer finds (12.5\% improvement demonstrated on Greater Miranda --- see Section~\ref{sec:sa-trace}).

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{SA Post-Processing}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Partition from BFS, temperature schedule $T_0 \to T_f$
\FOR{$\text{iter} = 1$ to 500}
    \STATE $k_{\max} \leftarrow \arg\max_k T_k$; select boundary segment $s$
    \STATE $k_{\text{target}} \leftarrow$ adjacent ESW with lowest $T_k$
    \STATE Transfer $s$: $k_{\max} \to k_{\text{target}}$
    \STATE $\Delta \leftarrow \max_k T_k^{\text{new}} - \max_k T_k^{\text{old}}$
    \IF{$\Delta < 0$ or $\text{rand}() < e^{-\Delta / T}$}
        \STATE Accept transfer
    \ELSE
        \STATE Reject; undo transfer
    \ENDIF
    \STATE Cool: $T \leftarrow T \cdot \alpha$
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Oracle}
\label{sec:oracle}

Since no single strategy dominates, the oracle runs all strategies and selects the best by score (Eq.~\ref{eq:score}).

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Oracle}
\label{alg:oracle}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Segments $\mathcal{S}$, worker count $N$
\STATE Run Chain+Slice, Right-Hand, 2-Opt, Bisect, Voronoi, BFS, DFS, BFS+SA
\STATE Score each by $2 \cdot W_{\max} + W_{\text{total}} + 5000 \cdot \text{imbalance}$
\RETURN partition with lowest score
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\textbf{Runtime.} For $M$ segments and $I$ intersection nodes: Dijkstra precomputation is $O(I \cdot (I + E) \log I)$ where $E$ is edge count (one-time, 2--41\,s in practice). The oracle body is $O(M^3)$ for each chain strategy (dominated by $M$ starting segments $\times$ $M^2$ chain construction). For neighborhoods up to 289 segments, the oracle completes in $<$1\,s after precomputation.

\subsection{Scoring Function}
\label{sec:scoring}

Candidate assignments are scored by:
\begin{equation}
\text{Score} = 2 \cdot W_{\max} + W_{\text{total}} + 5000 \cdot \frac{\sum_k ||P_k| - A/N|}{A/N}
\label{eq:score}
\end{equation}

The three terms serve distinct purposes:
\begin{itemize}
\item $2 \cdot W_{\max}$: \textbf{Fairness.} The worst-case ESW walk, weighted 2$\times$ because the objective (Eq.~\ref{eq:objective}) is to minimize the maximum.
\item $W_{\text{total}}$: \textbf{Efficiency.} Total distance across all ESWs. Two assignments with the same $W_{\max}$ are distinguished by total effort.
\item $5000 \cdot \text{imbalance}$: \textbf{Balance penalty.} The constant 5000 was chosen empirically so that a 10\% address-count imbalance adds 500\,m equivalent --- comparable to one inter-segment transition. This prevents grossly unbalanced partitions while allowing minor imbalance if it reduces walking.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Random Baseline}

Shuffle segments, partition by address count, NN chain within each group. Run 30 trials, keep best. Quantifies the value of optimization: the oracle improves over random by 37\% on average.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Random Baseline}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Segments $\mathcal{S}$, worker count $N$, trials $R = 30$
\STATE $\text{best} \leftarrow \infty$
\FOR{$r = 1$ to $R$}
    \STATE Randomly shuffle $\mathcal{S}$
    \STATE Partition into $N$ groups at $\lceil A/N \rceil$ address boundaries
    \STATE Order each group by NN chain
    \STATE Score partition; update best if improved
\ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Simulation Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
%% ==================================================================

\textbf{Data pipeline.} OpenStreetMap data is imported via \texttt{osm2pgsql} from a Geofabrik California PBF (1.3\,GB), filtered to the Palo Alto bounding box. Road segments are split at intersection nodes. Municipal addresses are snapped to segments by perpendicular projection. Cost matrices are precomputed from address positions and street widths (estimated from OSM road class).

\textbf{Distance computation.} Dijkstra shortest paths are precomputed between all intersection nodes within each neighborhood (2--31\,s depending on size). All inter-segment distances use road-network paths, not haversine.

\textbf{Parameters.} As specified in the general constraints (Section~\ref{sec:formulation}): $t_{\text{assess}} = 5$\,min/address, $v = 5$\,km/h, huddle at nearest intersection to serving fire station.

\textbf{Test matrix.} 10 algorithms $\times$ 5 neighborhoods $\times$ $N \in \{3, 5, 7, 10\}$ = 200 test cases. Each evaluated with all three metrics. All algorithms use the same segment data, distance matrices, huddle points, and evaluation function. The walk from huddle to first segment is included in every algorithm's unproductive distance --- even for algorithms (e.g., chain+slice) that do not explicitly model the starting location during construction.

\textbf{Haversine vs.\ road-network validation.} To validate the road-network model, we compare algorithm rankings under haversine distances vs.\ road-network distances. Under haversine, transition distances are straight-line. In our earlier haversine-based study across 41 neighborhoods, bisect dominated at $N = 5$ (21/41 wins) and NN chain at $N = 3$ (18/41). Under road-network distances, the ranking shifts: 2-opt and bisection share dominance, NN chain drops to 7\%. Haversine underestimates the cost of transitions that must navigate around blocks (Fig.~\ref{fig:diag-road} shows a 56\% underestimate), validating the constrained model.

\subsection{Overhead Decomposition}

The overhead ratio $\rho$ has two sources: (1) unproductive walking (transitions), and (2) productive imbalance (some ESWs traverse more segment distance than others). We can decompose:

\begin{equation}
\rho = \frac{W_{\max}}{W^*} = \frac{W_{\max}^{\text{prod}} + W_{\max}^{\text{unprod}}}{W^*}
\end{equation}

For Fairmeadow ($\rho = 1.60$): $W^* = 2{,}236$\,m, $W_{\max} = 3{,}571$\,m. The worst-case ESW has $\sim$2{,}400\,m productive and $\sim$1{,}171\,m unproductive. If transitions could be eliminated entirely, $\rho$ would drop to $2{,}400/2{,}236 = 1.07$ --- the remaining 7\% overhead is from productive imbalance (some ESWs get slightly longer segments). For Monroe Park ($\rho = 2.77$): eliminating transitions would give $\rho \approx 1.5$, meaning 65\% of the overhead is from transitions and 35\% from imbalance.

\textbf{Implication:} The path to reducing $\rho$ is primarily through smarter transition routing (e.g., solving a TSP on segment endpoints), not through better partitioning.

\subsection{Time-Based Analysis from Huddle Points}
\label{sec:time-results}

We also evaluate all algorithms under the time metric (Eq.~\ref{eq:finish-time}), with all workers starting from shared huddle points. The key findings:

\textbf{Assessment time dominates.} At $t_{\text{assess}} = 5$\,min, assessment time is $\sim$8$\times$ walking time for typical neighborhoods. The time metric is therefore dominated by address count balance, which chain+slice handles naturally through its $\lceil A/N \rceil$ slicing.

\textbf{BFS produces operationally unacceptable paths.} BFS+SA, despite producing reasonable time balance, generates \emph{twice the total walking distance} of DFS or chain+slice (21.4\,km vs.\ 10.2--12.5\,km on Fairmeadow). The BFS fan-out pattern interleaves ESW territories geographically, forcing extensive unproductive transitions. From an operational standpoint, ESWs walking past unvisited addresses to reach their assigned territory would find this unacceptable regardless of what the time metric says. This reveals that \textbf{walking quality} must be treated as a constraint, not just a component of the objective.

\textbf{DFS produces the best of both worlds} on compact neighborhoods: the lowest max time (575\,min on Fairmeadow), balanced addresses (105/102/105), and the least total walking (10.2\,km). But it suffers from the ``dregs problem'' on irregular neighborhoods where the last ESW inherits scattered remnants.

\subsection{Voronoi Analysis}
\label{sec:voronoi-results}

Voronoi partitioning with balanced seeds achieves 51\% walk quality average (range 22--75\%) and 99\,min average spread. This crosses the 50\% quality threshold, meaning ESWs spend more time visiting addresses than transitioning. The comparison with naive Lloyd's iteration is dramatic: Lloyd's produces 41\% walk quality and 193\,min spread --- the same Voronoi framework with different seed optimization yields fundamentally different results. Seed placement matters far more than the assignment metric.

Regular grid neighborhoods achieve near-perfect balance: Fairmeadow 107/104/101 ($N = 3$, 12\,min spread). Irregular layouts (Research Park) still produce higher spreads because the Voronoi partition cannot follow the non-uniform address density.

\subsection{Constraint Tension Analysis}
\label{sec:constraint-results}

We formulate all discovered constraints simultaneously and attempt to solve within them:

\textbf{Hard constraints:}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Walkable contiguity}: Each ESW's segments form a walk-ordered chain.
\item \textbf{Walking quality floor}: $Q_k \geq Q_{\min} = 50\%$ for each ESW.
\item \textbf{Full coverage}: Every segment assigned to exactly one ESW.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Objective}: Minimize finish-time spread subject to constraints 1--3.

\textbf{Result: only 6\% of cases are feasible.} With $Q_{\min} = 50\%$ across 19 neighborhoods at $N = 3, 5$ (36 test cases), SA rejects nearly all transfers because moving a boundary segment creates long unproductive transitions that violate the quality floor.

The infeasibility is structural. The median walking quality across all ESWs is only 24\% --- meaning 76\% of walking is transitions. The dominant factor is the \textbf{walk from the fire station huddle to the neighborhood}, which is unavoidable overhead. For neighborhoods far from their fire station (e.g., Southgate: 1.6\,km to Station~1), this single walk consumes most of the walking budget.

\textbf{The fundamental tension:} Walkable contiguity + time balance is achievable (chain+slice produces 70\,min average spread). Walking quality $\geq 50\%$ requires that most walking is productive, which is impossible when the huddle-to-neighborhood distance is large. The resolution depends on operational context: if ESWs are \emph{driven} to the neighborhood, the walk-to-first vanishes and walking quality jumps. If they walk from the station, the quality floor must be relaxed.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% ==================================================================

\subsection{When Each Algorithm Wins}

The five diverse neighborhoods partition the algorithm landscape:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Dense, compact (PAC):} Chain methods dominate (76\% WQ). All segments are adjacent; transitions are inherently short. Voronoi and Bisect struggle because any partition boundary creates unnecessary crossings in this small cluster.
\item \textbf{Cul-de-sacs (FM):} 2-opt wins (73\%) by refining chain ordering to follow the radial topology. Voronoi is competitive at large $N$ (80\% at $N = 10$) as cells align with cul-de-sac clusters.
\item \textbf{Regular grid (CC):} All methods perform similarly (61--71\% WQ). The grid aligns with both chain ordering and Voronoi boundaries. 2-opt's local search provides a small edge.
\item \textbf{Sparse campus (RP):} Everything fails ($<$30\% WQ). The fundamental problem is not the algorithm but the data: 289 segments with only 584 addresses means 2.0 addresses per segment --- most walking is between segments with no addresses.
\item \textbf{Elongated (SG):} Voronoi wins decisively (74\% vs.\ 52\% for 2-opt). The Euclidean partition naturally bisects the elongated shape. Chain methods snake through the length, producing interleaved partitions.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Productive-Distance Invariant}

Table~\ref{tab:master} confirms that productive distance is invariant ($\pm$5\%) across all algorithms within each neighborhood. This is the paper's deepest insight: \textbf{algorithms cannot reduce work --- they can only reduce waste.} The practical implication: when evaluating a new algorithm, compare its \emph{unproductive} distance to the best known, not its total distance.

\subsection{Walk-Ordered Sequences vs.\ Geographic Partitions}

Across all analyses, algorithms that build assignments as walk-ordered sequences of segments (chain+slice, 2-opt, DFS) consistently outperform those that partition geographic space first (BFS, Voronoi, bisect at small $N$). The reason: walking is inherently sequential --- an ESW visits one address, then the next, along a street. Algorithms that respect this sequential structure produce lower unproductive walking because consecutive segments in the chain are geographically adjacent.

\subsection{Recommended Practice}

The analyses converge on a concrete operational procedure:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Transport ESWs to the neighborhood.} The constraint tension analysis (Section~\ref{sec:constraint-results}) shows that the walk from fire station to neighborhood dominates walking quality, making the 50\% quality floor infeasible in 94\% of cases. Driving ESWs to a fan-out point at the neighborhood edge eliminates this overhead and makes all other constraints achievable.

\item \textbf{Run the oracle at the fan-out point.} Chain+slice with 2-opt, starting from the fan-out point, produces walk-ordered, geographically contiguous assignments in under one second. The oracle tries all strategies and selects the best by score.

\item \textbf{Check finish-time spread.} Compute $T_k$ for each ESW (Eq.~\ref{eq:finish-time}) with the NPC's estimated $t_{\text{assess}}$. If the spread exceeds the NPC's tolerance (e.g., 30 minutes), manually transfer one or two boundary segments from the slowest ESW to the fastest.

\item \textbf{Brief ESWs using the trail maps.} Show each ESW their walking trail (blue = productive segments, red = transitions). ESWs who can see that their route is logical and efficient --- mostly blue, minimal red --- will work with confidence rather than frustration.
\end{enumerate}

This procedure addresses every constraint discovered: walkable contiguity (chain+slice), time balance (oracle scoring + manual adjustment), walking quality (transport to fan-out), and ESW morale (trail visualization).

\subsection{Limitations}

\textbf{Single city.} All neighborhoods are in Palo Alto --- an affluent suburb with regular grids, cul-de-sacs, and flat terrain. Results may differ in dense urban, rural, or irregular-street environments.

\textbf{No field validation.} ESWs have not yet been observed using these assignments. A structured field trial is the most important next step.

\textbf{Equal service time.} All addresses are treated equally. Multi-unit buildings and high-rises require different assessment times, which the time metric could accommodate with per-address weights.

\textbf{Flat terrain.} Walking speed is assumed constant (5\,km/h). In hilly environments, a time-based metric with elevation-adjusted speed would be needed.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ==================================================================

The Capacitated Vehicle Routing Problem (CVRP)~\cite{toth2014} distributes service locations among vehicles from a depot. Our problem has no depot, requires balance, and uses walking-speed traversal.

Postal delivery route optimization~\cite{eiselt1995} is the most closely analogous domain: letter carriers walk along street segments visiting addresses on both sides, with route design that must balance workload and minimize deadheading (our ``unproductive walking''). Our cost matrix formulation is equivalent to the carrier's side-of-street decision at each block. Census enumeration area delineation~\cite{taylor2012} similarly partitions addresses into balanced geographic groups, though without the sequential ordering requirement.

K-means~\cite{lloyd1982} and balanced variants~\cite{malinen2014} cluster geographically but ignore street connectivity. The nearest-neighbor TSP heuristic~\cite{rosenkrantz1977} produces $O(\log n)$-competitive tours. Our within-segment problem is a special case of the TSP on parallel lines~\cite{frederickson1978}.

Recursive bisection~\cite{berger1987, karypis1998} is standard for balanced spatial decomposition. Our application to road-network segments with port-aware traversal costs appears novel. Simulated annealing~\cite{kirkpatrick1983} provides the post-optimization framework.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ==================================================================

We presented a practical system for assigning addresses to emergency services workers that minimizes worst-case walking distance using road-network-constrained routing. We summarize the evidence for each contribution:

\textbf{C1 (Segment model).} Decomposing the OSM road network into 64,482 segments with geometric address snapping (41,854 addresses, median snap distance 22.9\,m, 99\% within 50\,m) provides a physically accurate model of how ESWs walk. The diagnostic figures (Figs.~\ref{fig:diag-subseg}--\ref{fig:diag-road}) verify geometric correctness.

\textbf{C2 (Cost matrix).} The $4 \times 4$ matrix captures zigzag, U-turn, through-walk, and out-and-back in a unified formulation. 97\% of segments favor zigzag; the 3\% favoring U-turn are identified automatically. Port choice produces 74\% cost variation on a single segment (Section~\ref{sec:example}), justifying the matrix.

\textbf{C3 (Three metrics).} Walk quality, time spread, and the productive-distance invariant capture what matters operationally. The invariant reveals that \textbf{46\% of walking is unproductive on average}, confirming that inter-segment transition reduction is the dominant optimization target. Road-following distances average 1.3--1.6$\times$ haversine (Fig.~\ref{fig:diag-road}), validating the constrained model.

\textbf{C4 (Empirical evaluation).} Across 5 diverse neighborhoods at $N = 3, 5, 7, 10$ (200 test cases), no single algorithm dominates. The winning strategy depends on neighborhood geometry: 2-opt on compact/grid (76\%), Voronoi on elongated (74\%), all fail on sparse ($<$30\%).

\textbf{C5 (Oracle algorithm).} Running all strategies completes in $<$1\,s for neighborhoods up to 289 segments, enabling real-time use during emergency operations.

\textbf{C6 (Time-based analysis).} When assessment time dominates walking ($\sim$8$\times$), chain+slice still produces the best assignments because its contiguous geographic partitions naturally minimize unproductive walking. BFS produces twice the walking of DFS/chain+slice, revealing that walking quality must be a constraint.

\textbf{C7 (Voronoi with balanced seeds).} Balanced seed search transforms Voronoi from a failure (41\% walk quality, 193\,min spread with Lloyd's) into a competitive approach (51\% walk quality, 99\,min spread). Seed placement matters far more than the assignment metric.

\textbf{C8 (Constraint tension).} Walkable contiguity + time balance + walking quality $\geq$ 50\% is feasible in only 6\% of cases. The walk from the fire station to the neighborhood dominates walking quality (median 24\%). The resolution is operational: transport ESWs to the neighborhood.

\textbf{Cross-study insight.} The productive-distance invariant reveals that algorithms can only reduce waste, not work. This reframes future research: the objective is not ``better routing'' but ``less unproductive walking.'' Promising directions include Voronoi + chain+slice hybrids (Voronoi for geographic partitioning, chain+slice for intra-cell routing), constraint-solver formulations that explicitly balance walk quality and time spread, and field validation with ESW teams.

Future work includes: incorporating ESW transport (drive vs.\ walk to neighborhood) as a decision variable; adaptive $Q_{\min}$ based on huddle-to-neighborhood distance; field validation with ESW teams; and testing on cities with different street geometries.
